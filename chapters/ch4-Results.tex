\subsection{Causality}
\SetPicSubDir{ch4-Results/causality}

Figure \autoref{fig:partialR2} shows the partial R2 based on Static and Realtime group comfort models, along different number of sample K at session, showed in the three \ac{ml} model.
The results indicate that in the small number of session such as 1 to 6, the Realtime group comfort models' model convergence is superior than that of Static group comfort models.
Within k = 4 to 5, Static group model's partial R2 become close to 0, while Realtime group model's partial R2 is around 0.1, which indicates that Static group comfort models doesn't show additional information rather than environmental and personal information, while Realtime group comfort model shows additional information.
When the number of sampled K increase to 7 to 8, Realtime group comfort models' Partial R2 get close to similar level as Static group comfort models nearly zero, which indicates that the Realtime group comfort model's additional information is not significant when the number of session is large enough.

    \caption{Partial R2}

These results indicate that Realtime group comfort models are able to show consistency with actual comfort vote when the occupancy count at the time is smaller than 6, however, its consistency converge to zero when occupancy count is close to 8.

In contrast, the improvement of real-time group comfort models and daily group comfort model can maintain a certain level of improvement even with maximum room user size, which ranges 6 to 10.
The difference between using daily group comfort models and realtime comfort models get wider as the room size get larger.
This indicates that with larger room size, the real-time group comfort models benefit with more dynamic occupant transitions, while daily group comfort models are not able to capture the dynamic group comfort tendency with occupancy transitions.
