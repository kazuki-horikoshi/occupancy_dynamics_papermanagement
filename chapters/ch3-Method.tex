\label{sec:Method}
This study explores the occupant comfort performance of \ac{occ} employing three types of \ac{gcm} based on different occupancy resolution and the relationship between occupancy parameters and the control.
To examine these, agentic-simulation framework is used based on data collected in a real-world office building.
The overall procedural flow of the study is illustrated in \autoref{fig:ex_flow}.

\subsection{Data Collection}
\label{subsec:DataCollect}
For the analysis, three primary types of field data are collected in the target building: real-time occupancy logs, subjective thermal comfort surveys and environmental measurement logs.

\subsubsection{Target Building and Spaces}
\label{sec:TargetBuil}
The data collection was conducted at The GEAR, an R\&D and administrative office building located in Singapore, from 29 September to 24 October 2025 (4 weeks).
The 3rd to 6th floors, which accommodate a mix of shared-office and static seating work styles depending on departmental roles, are the target areas for data collection.
Data collection was limited to the following rooms to capture a variety of occupancy patterns:
3F: Office 3A, Office 3B; 5F: Katris Office, KD Office, Shared Office 1, Shared Office 2; 6F: ILYA Office.

The target zones are served by a central air conditioning system consistting of air-handling unit and dedicated outdoor air handling unit, using variable air volume (VAV) capable of individual zone control.
The controlled variable is the zone air temperature setpoint ($T_{\mathrm{set}}$). 

\subsubsection{Thermal Comfort Survey}
Subjective \ac{tcv} survey answers were collected via a brief web survey during work hours. 
Items and scales follow ANSI/ASHRAE Standard 55:2023, Appendix~L \cite{ashrae_ansiashrae_2022}.
The survey contents are summarized in \autoref{table:comfsurvey}.
Responses within 20 minutes of room entry were excluded to avoid thermal transient effects.
Participants were prompted up to six times per workday, three times in morning and three times in afternoon.
Over the one-month observation period, more than 2000 survey responses were collected in total.

\subsubsection{Indoor Environmental Measurements}
Indoor environmental data are monitored in parallel with \ac{tcv} survey data.
The primary measurements were \ac{ti} and \ac{rh}, obtained from the \ac{bms} and compact sensors installed in the target rooms.
To ensure sufficient variability of survey answers, indoor \ac{ti} setpoints were cycled between \qtyrange{22}{27}{\celsius} in \qty{1}{\celsius} steps, with randomized order across days.  
On selected days, morning and afternoon setpoints differed to expand diversity.  
In addition, measured air speed (\acs{v}) and mean radiant temperature (\acs{tr}) were recorded periodically as diagnostic checks rather than as main focus of the analysis.

\subsubsection{Individual-level location tracking log}
Individual-level location tracking log are reconstructed from anonymized, timestamped location data obtained from the \ac{bms}.
From a raw stream of zone-level access events, a structured “stay log” is generated to represent continuous periods of presence for each participant in each room.
The resulting log, which contains an anonymized ID, location, and start–end times, is then used to compute occupancy metrics.
The detailed procedure for this reconstruction is described in~\autoref{app:occupancy_reconstruction}.

\subsubsection{Participant Recruitment and Ethical Considerations}
45 office workers who regularly work in the designated study rooms were invited to participate.
This study has been approved by the National University of Singapore Institutional Review Board (NUS-IRB Reference Code: NUS-IRB-2025-498).
Informed consent were obtained from all participants prior to the study.
All location and survey data were anonymized by the building administrator before being accessed by the research team to protect privacy. 
Considering the actual survey amount, survey answers and location tracking data of 39 participants are finally used. 

\subsection{Occupancy-log-based agentic comfort simulation}
\subsubsection{Simulation Design}
Using collected \ac{tcv} data associated with environmental logs and location logs, \ac{pcm}s and location-log agent are constructed respectivelyfor each participant.
Using these \ac{pcm}s and location-agents, a Monte Carlo-style repeated resampling simulation is conducted.
Each location-agent is treated as an occupant proxy carrying an observed room-level presence record reconstructed from the stay logs over working days of one month.
\textbf{The sample is shown in appendix.}

Using location-agents, dynamic room occupant compositions and optmal temperature control based on different resolutions of \ac{gcm} generated by corresponding \ac{pcm}s are simulated.
Using these dyanmic occupant compositions and \ac{gcm}s, three types of \ac{occ} policies are evaluated from comfort and control perspectives.

\subsubsection{Personal Comfort Model (PCM)}
Individual \acp{pcm} are developed for each participant to predict thermal preference based on \ac{ti}, which is sourced from compact indoor environment sensors located in the target room and the mean value of sensora is used to reduce senso-specific measuremet bias.
The \ac{pcm}s are developed using multinominal logistic classification, used in similar agentic-based thermal comfort simulations~\cite{jung_comparative_2019, jung_energy_2020}.
The model predicts the probability distribution over three thermal preference classes: $S_{th} \in \{+1 (\text{Warmer}), 0 (\text{No change}), -1 (\text{Cooler})\}$.
\begin{equation}
P(S_{th} = s \mid t_i)\,.
\label{eq:pcm_probability}
\end{equation}

\subsubsection{Three-level time-resolution \ac{gcm}s}
This study compares three \acp{gcm} that differ in the timescale at which individual \acp{pcm} are aggregated: a \ac{sgcm}, a \ac{dgcm}, and a \ac{rtgcm}.
The \ac{sgcm} aggregates the \acp{pcm} of the room's registered regular members and yields one fixed room-level representation over the study period.
The \ac{dgcm} aggregates the \acp{pcm} of the occupants who attended on a given day and is updated at the daily timescale.
The \ac{rtgcm} aggregates the \acp{pcm} of the occupants actually present at each control update and is therefore the highest-resolution representation.

\subsection{Agentic simulation}
\begin{figure}[pos=!bt]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.97\linewidth]{\Pic{png}{OverallFlow}}
    \caption{Overall flow of simulation. Step1 (a)From room user pool, a subgroup of agents is sampled and PCMs are randomly assigned to the sampled agents. Step2(c) Based on the assigned PCMs and location logs,(d) Possibility curve of "No change" in thermal preference id merged based on three types of GCM at each control timestep (d) Setpoints are selected at the point where the aggregated comfort probability is maximized (e) The setpoints are evaluated by returning to the assigned PCMs and extracting the predicted ``No change'' probability from each PCMs}
    \label{fig:ComChara}

Using \ac{pcm} and location-log agents, various combination of two proxies are tested over target rooms.
To generate multiple realizations while preserving the original room usage patterns, subgroups of agents with moving logs are repeatedly sampled from each room's member pool, and individual \acp{pcm} are randomly reassigned to the selected proxies in each trial.
Using the location-agents, room occupant compositions are simulated. 
Each simulation trial first samples a subgroup of agents from the room member pool.
The sampled agents retain their observed movement records, while \acp{pcm} are randomly assigned to the sampled agents in each trial.
At each timestamp $t$, the subset of sampled agents who are present in room $r$ is denoted by $S_{r,t}$.

For each simulated realization, the three \acp{gcm} explained in the previous section are compared by aggregating the assigned \acp{pcm} at each timescale resolutions.
Each group's comfort representation is formed by merging the individual ``No change'' probability curves of the assigned \acp{pcm}, and the corresponding setpoint is selected as the temperature that maximizes the aggregated comfort probability as formed below.

Let $p_i(T)=P_i(S_{th}=0\mid T)$ denote the ``No change'' probability predicted by the \ac{pcm} assigned to agent $i$ at temperature $T$.
For a group of agents $G$, the merged group comfort probability is defined as
\begin{equation}
\bar{p}_{G}(T)
=
\frac{1}{|G|}
\sum_{i\in G} p_i(T).
\label{eq:gcm_probability_curve}
\end{equation}
The group-level setpoint is then selected as
\begin{equation}
T_G^{*}
=
\operatorname*{arg\,max}_{T\in\mathcal{T}_{\mathrm{set}}}
\bar{p}_{G}(T),
\label{eq:gcm_optimal_setpoint}
\end{equation}
where $\mathcal{T}_{\mathrm{set}}$ is the temperature grid of candidate setpoints.
The policy-specific groups are
\begin{equation}
G_{r}^{\mathrm{Static}} = R_r,
\qquad
G_{r,d}^{\mathrm{Daily}} = A_{r,d},
\qquad
G_{r,t}^{\mathrm{RT}} = S_{r,t},
\label{eq:policy_groups}
\end{equation}
where $R_r$ is the sampled room member group for room $r$, $A_{r,d}$ is the set of sampled agents who appear in room $r$ on day $d$, and $S_{r,t}$ is the set of sampled agents present at timestamp $t$.
The resulting policy setpoints are
\begin{equation}
T_{\mathrm{set}}^{\mathrm{Static}}(t)=T_{G_{r}^{\mathrm{Static}}}^{*},
\qquad
T_{\mathrm{set}}^{\mathrm{Daily}}(t)=T_{G_{r,d(t)}^{\mathrm{Daily}}}^{*},
\qquad
T_{\mathrm{set}}^{\mathrm{RT}}(t)=T_{G_{r,t}^{\mathrm{RT}}}^{*},
\label{eq:policy_setpoints}
\end{equation}
where $d(t)$ is the calendar day containing timestamp $t$.

In addition to these three group-model-based policies, a fixed \qty{24}{\celsius} setpoint is used as an additional baseline.
For each room and subgroup-size, \qty{1000} combinations of \ac{pcm} and location log agents are formed and one-month OCC simulations based on three types of \ac{gcm} are conducted.


\subsection{Evaluation Metrics}
\subsubsection{Comfort-Related}
\label{subsec:comfMetrics}

Let $T_{\mathrm{set}}^{\mathrm{policy}}(t)$ denote the setpoint at time $t$ generated by policy $\mathrm{policy}\in\{\mathrm{Static},\mathrm{Daily},\mathrm{Dynamic}\}$.

For each present agent $i\in S_{r,t}$, the setpoint generated by a policy is evaluated by returning to the assigned personal model and extracting the predicted ``No change'' probability:
\begin{equation}
q_{i,t}^{\mathrm{policy}}
=
p_i\!\left(T_{\mathrm{set}}^{\mathrm{policy}}(t)\right).
\label{eq:comfort_probability_score}
\end{equation}
The room-day comfort probability is then computed as the person-time average:
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{CP}_{r,d}^{\mathrm{policy}}
=
\frac{
\sum_{t\in\mathcal{T}_{r,d}}
\sum_{i\in S_{r,t}}
q_{i,t}^{\mathrm{policy}}
}{
\sum_{t\in\mathcal{T}_{r,d}}
|S_{r,t}|
}.
\label{eq:comfort_probability_daily}
\end{equation}
Here, $\mathcal{T}_{r,d}$ is the ordered set of analysis timestamps for room $r$ on day $d$.
Timestamps with no present agents do not contribute person-time comfort scores.

\subsubsection{Evaluation Metrics: Occupancy Related}
\label{sec:OccuMetrics}
\autoref{table:OccuMetrics} in~\autoref{app:OccupancyStudy} summarizes occupancy-related literature and metrics after COVID-19.
While many studies emphasize occupancy count and related parameters, this study also considers variability parameters that reflect individual differences in preferred setpoints.
Let $\mathcal{T}_{r,d}$ denote the ordered set of analysis timestamps for room $r$ on day $d$, and let $S_{r,t}$ denote the set of sampled agents present in room $r$ at timestamp $t$.
The realized occupancy count is $N_{r,t}=|S_{r,t}|$.
The subgroup size $K$ is the number of sampled agents in a simulation condition, whereas $N_{r,t}$ is the number of those agents actually present at timestamp $t$.

\paragraph{Occupancy count (target group size effect).}
\begin{equation}
N_{r,t} \;=\; |S_{r,t}|.
\label{eq:Nt}
\end{equation}
The daily mean occupancy is
\begin{equation}
\bar{N}_{r,d}
=
\frac{1}{|\mathcal{T}_{r,d}|}
\sum_{t\in\mathcal{T}_{r,d}} N_{r,t}.
\label{eq:mean_occ_daily}
\end{equation}
Larger realized groups average out individual preferences more strongly and are expected to reduce the difference between low- and high-resolution group models.

\paragraph{Occupancy variability (variability of occupancy count).}
To capture within-day fluctuations in room load, the daily standard deviation of realized occupancy count is used:
\begin{equation}
\sigma_{N,r,d}
=
\sqrt{
\frac{1}{|\mathcal{T}_{r,d}|-1}
\sum_{t\in\mathcal{T}_{r,d}}
\left(N_{r,t}-\bar{N}_{r,d}\right)^2
}.
\label{eq:occ_var}
\end{equation}
Greater variability indicates volatile attendance and is expected to increase the mismatch between fixed and real-time group aggregation.

\paragraph{\ac{utr}.}
The short-horizon replacement of attendees is quantified using a rolling Jaccard turnover, denoted as \ac{utr}.
For timestamp-level analysis, \ac{utr} is computed by comparing the current occupant set with the set observed 30 minutes earlier:
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{UTR}_{r,t}^{30}
=
1
-
\frac{
|S_{r,t}\cap S_{r,t-30}|
}{
|S_{r,t}\cup S_{r,t-30}|
}.
\label{eq:rolling_turnover}
\end{equation}
Here, $S_{r,t-30}$ denotes the occupant set in the same room 30 minutes before timestamp $t$.
When both the current and lagged sets are empty, the timestamp-level turnover is treated as undefined.
Higher $\mathrm{UTR}_{r,t}^{30}$ indicates stronger short-horizon replacement of attendees.

For daily summaries, timestamp-level \ac{utr} values are averaged within each room-day:
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{UTR}_{r,d}
=
\frac{1}{|\mathcal{V}_{r,d}|}
\sum_{t\in\mathcal{V}_{r,d}}
\mathrm{UTR}_{r,t}^{30},
\label{eq:utr_daily}
\end{equation}
where $\mathcal{V}_{r,d}$ is the set of timestamps on day $d$ for which the 30-minute turnover is defined.
The daily \ac{utr} captures how much the present group composition changes over short horizons, even when the occupancy count is similar.

\subsubsection{Control Related}
Among the three \acp{gcm} defined above, the \ac{rtgcm} setpoint $T_{\mathrm{set}}^{\mathrm{RT}}(t)$ is treated as the highest-resolution target and is compared with the \ac{sgcm} and \ac{dgcm} baselines.
The deviations are
\begin{equation}
\Delta T^{\mathrm{Static}}_t
\;=\;
T_{\mathrm{set}}^{\mathrm{RT}}(t)
-
T_{\mathrm{set}}^{\mathrm{Static}}(t),
\qquad
\Delta T^{\mathrm{Daily}}_t
\;=\;
T_{\mathrm{set}}^{\mathrm{RT}}(t)
-
T_{\mathrm{set}}^{\mathrm{Daily}}(t).
\label{eq:deltaT_defs}
\end{equation}
Unless stated otherwise, the magnitudes of $|\Delta T^{\mathrm{Static}}_t|$ and $|\Delta T^{\mathrm{Daily}}_t|$ will be analyzed.
