This study consists of two phase analysis, useing data collected in a real-world office building.
The first part is causality analysis which employees \ac{dml} to verify the consistency of different types of \ac{gcm} to actual comfort vote.
The second part employes agentic-simulation analysis to explore the relationship between occupancy-related parameters to the effect of control performance based on different types of \ac{gcm}.

\subsection{Collected Data}
\label{subsec:DataCollect}
For both analysis, three primary types of field data were collected: real-time occupancy logs, subjective thermal comfort surveys and environmental measurement logs.

\subsubsection{Occupancy Log Tracking}
Dynamic occupancy patterns will be reconstructed from anonymized, timestamped location data provided by the building's management system.
From a raw stream of access events, a structured “stay log” is generated to represent continuous periods of presence for each participant in each room.
The resulting log, which contains an anonymized ID, location, and start–end times, is then used to compute occupancy metrics.
The detailed procedure for this reconstruction is described in~\autoref{app:occupancy_reconstruction}.

\subsubsection{Thermal Comfort Survey}
Subjective survey answers were collected via a brief web survey during work hours. 
Items and scales follow ANSI/ASHRAE Standard 55:2023, Appendix~L \cite{ashrae_ansiashrae_2022}.
The survey contents are summarized in \autoref{table:comfsurvey}.

To ensure sufficient variability, indoor setpoints were cycled between \qtyrange{22}{27}{\celsius} in \qty{1}{\celsius} steps, with randomized order across days.  
On selected days, morning and afternoon setpoints differed to expand diversity.  
Participants were prompted with a target of 4–6 responses per workday.  
Responses within 20 minutes of room entry were excluded to avoid thermal transient effects.

\subsubsection{Indoor Environmental Measurements}
Indoor environmental data were monitored in parallel with occupancy and survey data.
The primary measurements were \ac{ti} and \ac{rh}, obtained from the \ac{bms} and compact sensors installed in the target rooms.
In addition, measured air speed (\acs{v}) and mean radiant temperature (\acs{tr}) were recorded periodically as diagnostic checks rather than as model inputs.

\subsection{Target Building and Spaces}
\label{sec:TargetBuil}
The data collection was conducted at The GEAR, an R\&D and administrative office building operated by Kajima Corporation, from 29 September to 24 October 2025 (4 weeks).
The 3rd to 6th floors, which accommodate a mix of ABW and static seating work styles depending on departmental roles, are the target areas for data collection.
Data collection was limited to the following rooms to capture a variety of occupancy patterns:
3F: Office 3A, Office 3B; 5F: Katris Office, KD Office, Shared Office 1, Shared Office 2; 6F: ILYA Office.

The target zones are served by a central air conditioning system using variable air volume (VAV) capable of individual zone control.
In this study, the controlled variable is the zone air temperature setpoint ($T_{\mathrm{set}}$). 
The \ac{bms} regulates the terminal unit such as airflow dampers or valve positions to maintain the measured \ac{ti} at the target $T_{\mathrm{set}}$.
$T_{\mathrm{set}}$ is manipulated experimentally to collect data.

\subsection{Participant Recruitment and Ethical Considerations}
45 office workers who regularly work in the designated study rooms were invited to participate.
This study has been approved by the National University of Singapore Institutional Review Board (NUS-IRB Reference Code: NUS-IRB-2025-498).
Informed consent were obtained from all participants prior to the study.
All location and survey data were anonymized by the building administrator before being accessed by the research team to protect privacy. 
Considering the actual survey amount, survey answers and location tracking data of 39 participants are finally used. 

\subsection{Analysis target Group Comfort Models}
This study compares three \acp{gcm} that differ in the timescale at which individual \acp{pcm} are aggregated: a \ac{sgcm}, a \ac{dgcm}, and a \ac{rtgcm}.
The \ac{sgcm} aggregates the \acp{pcm} of the room's registered regular members and yields one fixed room-level representation over the study period.
The \ac{dgcm} aggregates the \acp{pcm} of the occupants who attended on a given day and is updated at the daily timescale.
The \ac{rtgcm} aggregates the \acp{pcm} of the occupants actually present at each control update and is therefore the highest-resolution representation.

\subsection{Analysis 1:Response-Level Causality Analysis of Comfort Representations}
\label{subsec:causality_analysis}

\subsubsection{Double machine learning framework for comfort representation analysis.}
The objective of Analysis 1 is to evaluate whether different level of room-level comfort representations are informative of actual occupant comfort after accounting for the observed indoor and occupancy conditions.
For this purpose, a \ac{dml}-based residualization framework is adopted.
\autoref{fig:CausalPrfm}\autoref{fig:generalDML} illustrates the general idea of \ac{dml} in the context of this study.
\ac{dml} is a causal inference approach that separates the effect of interest from high-dimensional confounding by first estimating nuisance functions for the outcome and treatment, and then performing the target regression on their residual components\cite{chernozhukov_doubledebiased_2018}.
In the present study, this framework is used not to estimate the effect of a manipulated intervention, but to examine whether different \acp{gcm} remain associated with actual comfort beyond what can already be explained by the observed covariates.

Let $Y$ denote the observed comfort outcome for each survey response, and let $W$ denote the observed confounders, including indoor environmental conditions, clothing level, metabolic rate, and occupancy count.
Unlike a standard treatment-effect setting, the treatment variable $T$ here is a model-based comfort representation derived from \acp{pcm}.
More specifically, for each response or session, participant-level comfort probabilities are first estimated from fold-safe \acp{pcm}, and these probabilities are then aggregated to form group-level comfort representations such as the static, daily, and real-time \acp{gcm}.
Therefore, $T$ should be interpreted as a summarized comfort signal implied by a given group representation, rather than as an externally assigned intervention.

Under this formulation, the role of \ac{dml} is to remove the variation in both $Y$ and $T$ that is predictable from $W$.
This is done by estimating the nuisance functions $\widehat{E}[Y \mid W]$ and $\widehat{E}[T \mid W]$ using cross-fitting, and then constructing residuals
\[
Y^{\mathrm{res}} = Y - \widehat{E}[Y \mid W], \qquad
T^{\mathrm{res}} = T - \widehat{E}[T \mid W].
\]
The remaining association between $T^{\mathrm{res}}$ and $Y^{\mathrm{res}}$ is then interpreted as the component of the comfort representation that is not explained by the observed confounders.
In this study, the resulting coefficient is used as a measure of how consistently each \ac{gcm}-based representation aligns with actual comfort responses after adjustment for environmental and occupancy-related factors.

\subsubsection{Causality modeling}
To complement the occupancy-pattern and simulation analyses, a response-level comparison of alternative comfort representations are conducted using the field observations.
\autoref{fig:CausalPrfm}\autoref{fig:DMLflow} depicts the specific data and analysis flow for this comfort representation analysis.

The unit of analysis is an individual survey response.
A session is defined as the combination of date, merged time slot, and room.
The binary outcome, actual comfort, is defined as
\[
Y_{\mathrm{actual\_comfort}} = \mathbb{1}(\texttt{thermal\_preference\_num}=0).
\]
The main observed covariates are \ac{ti}, \ac{rh}, \ac{clo}, \ac{met}, and occupant count.
All sample splitting is performed with GroupKFold ($n=5$) using session ID as the grouping variable so that responses from the same session are never split across training and evaluation folds.

Several treatment representations are compared.
In parallel, participant-specific comfort probabilities are estimated from the environmental variables using fold-safe out-of-fold predictions from individual \acp{pcm}.
Depending on data availability, these \acp{pcm} are fit with participant-level classification models such as logistic regression, random forest, and multilayer perceptrons; when participant-specific training data are too sparse, predictions fall back to the training-fold mean and, if unavailable, to 0.5.
These individual predictions are then aggregated into group-level representations, including
(i) a real-time representation based on the mean prediction of the occupants actually observed in the same session, corresponding to an \ac{rtgcm};
(ii) a static representation based on the room's registered regular members evaluated under the session-average environment, corresponding to an \ac{sgcm};
and (iii) a subsession real-time representation based on exact-$k$ subsets of observed respondents within the same session.
For the subsession real-time representation, the candidate subset always includes the focal respondent and is constructed only from respondents actually observed in that session.

The estimation follows a cross-fitted residualization pipeline.
For each fold and each treatment representation $T$, nuisance functions $\widehat{E}[Y \mid W]$ and $\widehat{E}[T \mid W]$ are estimated, and residuals are constructed as
\[
Y^{\mathrm{res}} = Y_{\mathrm{actual\_comfort}} - \widehat{E}[Y \mid W], \qquad
T^{\mathrm{res}} = T - \widehat{E}[T \mid W].
\]
Treatment effects are then summarized by regressing $Y^{\mathrm{res}}$ on $T^{\mathrm{res}}$ with both intercept and no-intercept specifications.
The primary estimand is the residual-regression coefficient $\theta$, reported together with its standard error, $p$-value, 95\% confidence interval, and \ac{pr2}.
To reduce dependence on a single nuisance specification, additional reruns average outcome residuals across multiple cross-fitted models and random seeds.
Supplementary subgroup analyses also examine information-rich conditions in which the baseline predicted comfort probability is close to 0.5.


\subsection{Analysis 2:Occupancy-log-based agentic comfort simulation}
\subsubsection{Simulation Design}
A occupancy-log-based simulation is conducted along two phase.
At first phase, real office usage is simulated, using actual experiment participants' occupancy logs and comfort models to quantify the benefits of controls based-on each comfort representations.
As a second phase, a Monte Carlo-style repeated resampling analysis is conducted built on the observed room-level occupancy dynamics.
Each agent is treated as an occupant proxy carrying an observed presence record reconstructed from the stay logs.
To generate multiple realizations while preserving the original room usage patterns, subgroups are repeatedly sampled from each room's member pool, and individual \acp{pcm} are randomly reassigned to the selected proxies in each trial.

For each simulated realization, the three \acp{gcm} are compared by aggregating the assigned \acp{pcm} at the static, daily, and real-time timescales defined above.
More specifically, each group's comfort representation is formed by merging the individual "no change" probability curves of the assigned \acp{pcm}, and the corresponding setpoint is selected as the temperature that maximizes the aggregated comfort probability.
In addition to these three group-model-based policies, a fixed \qty{24}{\celsius} setpoint is used as an additional baseline.
The simulation outputs are then summarized as comfort improvement relative to this fixed baseline and as contrasts among the static, daily, and real-time group models

\subsubsection{Personal Comfort Model (PCM)}

Individual \acp{pcm} are developed for each participant to predict thermal preference based on indoor air temperature.
Environmental variables are sourced from compact indoor environment sensors (indoor air temperature (\acs{ti}).
The baseline \ac{pcm} are a multi-class logistic classification model, used in similar agentic-based thermal comfort simulations~\cite{jung_comparative_2019, jung_energy_2020}.

%study by Daum et al.~\cite{daum_personalized_2011}, widely adopted in prior studies~\cite{jung_comparative_2019,lee_implementation_2019,zhang_impact_2023,meimand_personal_2024,topak_collective_2023,gupta_incentive-based_2018}.

Specific model inputs and outputs are defined as follows:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Input vector $\mathbf{x}$:} The primary feature is indoor air temperature (\acs{ti}) obtained from the compact sensors nearest to the occupant's zone.
    \item \textbf{Output vector $\mathbf{y}$:} The model predicts the probability distribution over three thermal preference classes: $S_{th} \in \{+1 (\text{Warmer}), 0 (\text{No change}), -1 (\text{Cooler})\}$.
\end{itemize}

\[
P(S_{th} = s \mid t_i)\,.
\]

From each participant’s \ac{pcm}, individual metrics are derived:
\begin{description}
  \item[Preferred Temperature $T^p_i$:]
  The temperature that maximizes the probability of “no change,” i.e.,
  \[
    T^p_i = \operatorname*{arg\,max}_{t} P(S_{th}=0 \mid t)\,.
  \]
  \item[Individual Comfort Range {\([T^c_i,\,T^h_i]\)}:]
  The set of temperatures at which the probability of ``no change'' dominates the two preference alternatives (``warmer'' and ``cooler''):
  \[
  [T^c_i,\,T^h_i] \;\equiv\; \left\{\, t \;\middle|\; P(S_{th}=0 \mid t)\;\ge\;\max\!\bigl(P(S_{th}=+1\mid t),\,P(S_{th}=-1\mid t)\bigr) \,\right\}.
  \]
  This range will be used to evaluate how frequently a given setpoint schedule places present occupants within their personal comfort ranges.
\end{description}

\subsubsection{Evaluation Metrics: Comfort-Related}
\label{subsec:coverage}
% Objective: fraction of person-time within personal comfort ranges, without model-based smoothing.

\paragraph{Definition.}
Let $T_{\mathrm{set}}^{\mathrm{policy}}(t)$ denote the setpoint at time $t$ generated by policy $\mathrm{policy}\in\{\mathrm{Static},\mathrm{Daily},\mathrm{Dynamic}\}$.

For each occupant $i \in S_t$, define
\[
C_{i,t}^{\mathrm{policy}} =
\begin{cases}
1, & \text{if } T^c_i \le T_{\mathrm{set}}^{\mathrm{policy}}(t) \le T^h_i,\\[4pt]
0, & \text{otherwise.}
\end{cases}
\]

Room-level coverage over a target period $\mathcal{T}_r$ is
\[
\mathrm{Coverage}_{r}^{\mathrm{policy}}
=\frac{\sum_{t\in\mathcal{T}_r}\sum_{i\in S_t} w_{i,t}\, C_{i,t}^{\mathrm{policy}}}{\sum_{t\in\mathcal{T}_r}\sum_{i\in S_t} w_{i,t}},
\quad w_{i,t}=1\ \text{unless stated otherwise.}
\]

\paragraph{Occupancy-stratified summaries.}
To examine how occupancy conditions relate to coverage, stratified summaries will be reported by discretizing occupancy metrics into ordered bins
(e.g., quartiles): group size $N_t$, short-horizon count variability $\sigma^{(W)}_{N,t}$,
and user turnover rate $\mathrm{UTR}^{(W)}_t$ (Section~\ref{sec:OccuMetrics}).
For a bin $g$, define
\[
\mathrm{Coverage}_{r,g}^{\mathrm{policy}}
=\frac{\sum_{t\in\mathcal{T}_r \cap \mathcal{G}_g}\sum_{i\in S_t} C_{i,t}^{\mathrm{policy}}}{\sum_{t\in\mathcal{T}_r \cap \mathcal{G}_g}\sum_{i\in S_t} 1},
\]
and contrasts
\[
\begin{aligned} % align at "=" with two lines
\Delta \mathrm{Cov}_{r,g}^{\mathrm{Dyn-Daily}}
&= \mathrm{Coverage}_{r,g}^{\mathrm{Dynamic}} - \mathrm{Coverage}_{r,g}^{\mathrm{Daily}},\\
\Delta \mathrm{Cov}_{r,g}^{\mathrm{Dyn-Static}}
&= \mathrm{Coverage}_{r,g}^{\mathrm{Dynamic}} - \mathrm{Coverage}_{r,g}^{\mathrm{Static}}.
\end{aligned}
\]

\subsubsection{Evaluation Metrics: Occupancy Related}
\label{sec:OccuMetrics}
\autoref{table:OccuMetrics} in~\autoref{app:OccupancyStudy} summarizes occupancy-related literature and metrics after COVID-19.
While many studies emphasize occupancy count and related parameters, this study also considers variability parameters that reflect individual differences in preferred setpoints.

\paragraph{Occupancy count (target group size effect).}
\begin{equation}
N_t \;=\; |S_t|.
\label{eq:Nt}
\end{equation}
Larger groups average out individual preferences more strongly and are expected to reduce $|\Delta T|$.

\paragraph{Occupancy variability (variability of occupancy count).}
To capture short-horizon fluctuations in room load, a windowed dispersion of counts is used, e.g., for window size $W$,
\begin{equation}
\sigma^{(W)}_{N,t} \;=\; \operatorname{SD}\big(\,N_{t-W+1},\,\dots,\,N_t\,\big).
\label{eq:occ_var}
\end{equation}
Greater variability indicates volatile attendance and is expected to increase $|\Delta T|$.

\paragraph{\ac{utr}.}
The data are quantified in terms of short-horizon replacement of attendees via a rolling Jaccard turnover, denoted as \ac{utr}:
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{UTR}^{(W)}_t \;=\; 1 \;-\; \frac{|S_t \cap S_{t-W}|}{|S_t \cup S_{t-W}|}.
\label{eq:rolling_turnover}
\end{equation}
Higher $\mathrm{UTR}^{(W)}_t$ indicates more sustained replacement of attendees and is expected to increase $|\Delta T|$.

\subsubsection{Statistical Comparison and Equivalence Testing}
\label{subsec:equivalence}

It will be assessed whether the Dynamic setpoint is equivalent to each baseline using
two one-sided tests (TOST) with an equivalence margin of $\pm 0.5^\circ\mathrm{C}$
(aligned with the general thermostat control step). 
The null posits non-equivalence; rejection of both one-sided tests indicates equivalence under the specified occupancy conditions.

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Outcomes}
    Let $\Delta T^{\mathrm{Static}}_t$ and $\Delta T^{\mathrm{Daily}}_t$ be defined as in \eqref{eq:deltaT_defs}.
    Unless stated otherwise, the magnitudes of $|\Delta T^{\mathrm{Static}}_t|$ and $|\Delta T^{\mathrm{Daily}}_t|$ will be analyzed.
    \item \textbf{Hypotheses and margin}
    For each baseline, we test equivalence to Dynamic with the margin $\pm 0.5^\circ\mathrm{C}$ using TOST.

    \item \textbf{Procedure}
    Apply two one-sided tests at the pre-specified significance level; equivalence is concluded if both
    null hypotheses are rejected.

\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{Modeling strategy and Feature Attribution}

Because the relationship between occupancy metrics and the absolute deviations cannot be assumed linear, the absolute deviation $|\Delta T|$ will be modeled as a function of occupancy metrics:
\[
|\Delta T| \;=\; f\!\bigl(N_{\text{avg}},\, V_{\text{occ}},\, \mathrm{UTR}^{(W)}_t\bigr) \;+\; \varepsilon,
\]
where $f(\cdot)$ is approximated using Gaussian Process regression.
This allows us to capture potentially non-linear relationships between occupancy conditions and the deviation magnitude.

For interpretability, feature attribution methods such as SHAP (SHapley Additive exPlanations) will be applied to the GP predictions, so that the relative contribution of each occupancy metric ($N_{\text{avg}}, V_{\text{occ}}, \mathrm{UTR}^{(W)}_t$) can be identified.
This enables mapping of occupancy conditions where Dynamic control is equivalence-effective versus conditions where its advantage is limited.

\paragraph{Mapping Occupancy Conditions}

Based on the function above, two-dimensional mappings of occupancy metrics will be constructed to visualize the regions where Dynamic group control is equivalence-effective versus where its advantage is marginal.
This mapping provides actionable thresholds: for example, ranges of $N_{\text{avg}}$, $V_{\text{occ}}$, or $\mathrm{UTR}^{(W)}_t$
that delineate when Dynamic control should be prioritized in practice.
